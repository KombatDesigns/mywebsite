Wealth Management

Pension & Retirement

üñäÔ∏è Defined-benefit plans like HOOPP (Canada) or NHS Pension (UK) anchor hospital-employed physicians‚Äô retirement. Understand accrual rates, contribution tiers, annual allowance caps, and potential tax charges. Use pension estimators to model payments at different retirement ages and plan for inflation-adjusted income. For self-employed or private-practice doctors: RRSPs (18% of earned income to max $31,560 in 2024) and TFSAs offer tax-deferred or tax-free growth. An Individual Pension Plan (IPP) within a corporation allows older physicians higher deductible contributions, with actuarially determined benefits and potential surplus at wind-up. Factor in government benefits: CPP/QPP provides base income from age 60‚Äì70; OAS kicks in at 65 subject to clawback; US physicians should optimize Social Security by delaying benefits until full retirement age if possible. Use retirement calculators to combine defined-benefit, defined-contribution, and government streams, then adjust asset allocation‚Äîe.g., shifting from 70/30 equity/fixed-income in mid-career to 40/60 near retirement. Consider group RRSP/pension plans for staff, spousal RRSPs for income splitting, and carry-forward unused RRSP room. Review pension statements annually and consult a specialist to manage tapering allowances and maximize net retirement cash flow.

Billing & Revenue

üñäÔ∏èOntario physicians billing fee-for-service under OHIP rely on the Schedule of Benefits‚Äîeach code, premium, or modifier directly impacts practice revenue. Common revenue leaks include coding errors, missed modifiers (age, after-hours, outreach), and failure to bill all eligible services (e.g., chronic care management, team-based care premiums). Invest 4‚Äì6 hours monthly in claim reviews: reconcile remittances, correct rejections, and benchmark average visits per session. Alternatively, outsource billing to professional services or integrate with EMR-compatible software (OSCAR, Accuro, QHR) that auto-suggests codes and flags anomalies. Quarterly audits catch systemic issues‚Äîcompare expected vs. actual revenues, review documentation for high-value codes (surgical assists, consultation premiums), and ensure compliance with alternate models (FHO capitation vs. fee-for-service thresholds). Stay current via Ontario.ca Resources for Physicians, OMA briefings, and annual updates to the Schedule of Benefits. Leverage OMA courses, peer-mentorship, and third-party seminars to refine workflows. A small improvement‚Äîcapturing one extra modifier per day‚Äîcan yield thousands annually. Ethically accurate billing with robust documentation reduces audit risk and enhances practice profitability.

Professional Incorporation

üñäÔ∏è A Professional Corporation (PC) provides tax deferral on active business income at the small-business rate (11‚Äì13%) versus up to 50% personal. Surplus funds can be retained for corporate investments, compounding pre-tax returns. Be aware of passive income thresholds: corporate investment income above $50,000 reduces the small-business deduction. Income splitting through dividends to family members is restricted by TOSI rules‚Äîvalid only if family actively contribute to the practice. Salary versus dividend mix impacts CPP/QPP contribution room and RRSP eligibility; typically, pay minimal salary to preserve room and draw dividends for flexibility. Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE) applies on sale of PC shares (up to $1M+), beneficial upon practice sale or merger. Legal and accounting setup costs ($1‚Äì3K initially, plus annual compliance) mean incorporation benefits accrue when earnings exceed personal living needs. Advanced strategies include corporate bonus deferral (declared in one fiscal year, paid within 180 days), corporate-owned life insurance for tax-sheltered growth, and use of holding companies to segregate passive investments. Consult a corporate tax specialist to model scenarios.

Debt Management

üñäÔ∏è Canadian medical graduates average $90K+ in student loans; US grads often exceed $150K. Develop a repayment plan balancing debt payoff and investing. Attack high-interest debts (credit cards, lines of credit) immediately‚Äîuse bonuses or first-attending paycheques. For student loans, evaluate income-driven repayment plans and PSLF eligibility (US), or provincial rural/remote forgiveness programs (Canada). Calculate total cost under each option using specialized tools (AAMC MedLoans Organizer, NSLSC calculators). Once employed and credit improves, consider refinancing unsubsidized or private loans at lower rates‚Äîbut note loss of federal benefits if refinancing US loans. Maintain minimum payments on low-rate government loans while investing excess cash at expected portfolio returns (e.g., 5‚Äì7%). Use debt snowball (smallest balance first) or avalanche (highest rate first) methods to maintain motivation and efficiency. Track progress with visual tools to reinforce discipline. Avoid lifestyle creep; maintain a resident-like budget for one to two years post-training to accelerate payoff.

Tax Planning

üñäÔ∏è Maximize contributions to tax-advantaged accounts: RRSP (immediate deduction), TFSA (tax-free growth), HSA (pre-tax medical savings). Deduct business expenses‚ÄîCMPA fees, college dues, CME, home office, vehicle for on-call travel‚Äîby maintaining detailed logs. For incorporated physicians, structure salary/dividend mix to optimize corporate tax deductions, RRSP room generation, and personal tax credits. Use year-end bonus deferrals to smooth taxable income. Monitor GST/HST registration obligations for services outside publicly funded care. Internationally, navigate tax treaties for cross-border practice: CPT/TFSA recognition by IRS, departure taxes for emigrating doctors, and foreign tax credits. Leverage a physician-savvy CPA to file accurate returns and claim credits, avoiding penalties.

Estate Planning

üñäÔ∏è A comprehensive estate plan includes a valid will, executors, guardians for minors, and testamentary trusts to manage inheritance over time. Incorporate practice succession: buy-sell agreements funded by key-person life insurance ensure smooth transition and family compensation. Regularly update beneficiary designations for RRSP/RRIF, TFSA, and life policies‚Äîthese pass outside probate. Store documents securely and provide executors with access instructions. Review plans post major life events (marriage, divorce, practice sale).
